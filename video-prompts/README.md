# Video Prompts — 50 Free Copy-Paste Examples

## What is this?

This is a collection of 50 structured prompts for creating better video content — from finding ideas and writing scripts, to filming shot lists, YouTube SEO, short-form repurposing, and channel growth.

Most creators hit the same two walls: they cannot figure out what to film, or they film something and nobody watches it. Both problems are usually fixable — and AI can help with both if you give it the right prompt.

## The 5-Part Prompt Structure

Every prompt here uses a professional framework that tells the AI exactly who to be, what you need, and how to format the answer:

| Part | What it does |
|---|---|
| **ROLE** | Tells the AI who to be — a specific expert, not just "an assistant" |
| **CONTEXT** | Your situation — fill in the brackets with your real details |
| **TASK** | Numbered steps — exactly what you want the AI to produce |
| **CONSTRAINTS** | Rules on tone, format, and what to avoid |
| **OUTPUT FORMAT** | Exactly how the response should be structured |

**Plus a power footer** on every prompt — triggers the AI to ask for missing details first, think step by step, and suggest what to do next.

## How to use this

1. Open [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com), [Claude](https://claude.ai), or [Gemini](https://gemini.google.com) — all free
2. Find the section that matches where you are in your video creation process
3. Copy the full prompt including every section
4. Replace everything inside `[brackets]` with your real information
5. Paste and send

> **Works with:** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — for all scripts, ideas, hooks, descriptions, and strategy.
> For AI video generation (Runway, Sora, Pika), see the prompts in the [AI Image Prompts](../ai-image-prompts/) section as a starting point for visual direction.

## Video Idea & Concept Generation

*Start here — before you open a camera app, a script doc, or a title generator. The idea is everything.*

&nbsp;

**1.**
```
ROLE: You are a YouTube content strategist who has helped creators in competitive niches build channels from zero by finding angles nobody else is covering — not just what is popular, but what is searchable, specific, and actually watchable.

CONTEXT:
- My channel topic or niche: [e.g. personal finance for people in their 20s / watercolour painting for beginners / B2B SaaS marketing]
- My target audience: [who they are, what they want, what they already know]
- My channel size and stage: [e.g. just starting / 500 subscribers / 10k subscribers]
- Content I have already made: [list a few video topics, or "none yet"]
- Platforms I post on: [YouTube / TikTok / Instagram Reels / all three]

TASK:
1. Generate 10 specific video ideas for my niche — not generic titles, but fully formed concepts with a clear angle
2. For each idea: explain the search intent behind it, why someone would click it, and what would make it different from what already exists
3. Flag 3 ideas that are best for search traffic and 3 that are best for social sharing — explain the difference
4. Tell me which idea to make first and why

CONSTRAINTS:
- No generic ideas like "how to get started with X" unless there is a genuinely unique angle
- Every idea must have a clear specific viewer — not "beginners" but "beginners who already tried X and failed"
- At least 3 ideas should be counterintuitive or go against common advice in my niche

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Numbered list: Idea title → Target viewer → Why they click → What makes it different → Best for: search or social
- Bold the top 3 search ideas and mark them with [SEO]
- Bold the top 3 social ideas and mark them with [SOCIAL]
- End with: "Start here" pick + 3-sentence reasoning

> Before you start: If my niche or audience is too vague to generate specific ideas, ask me one question first. Generate ideas based on real search behaviour, not just what sounds interesting. When done, tell me: (1) one type of video format I haven't tried that would work well in my niche, and (2) the best next prompt to run to turn the top idea into a script.
```
*Produces 10 specific, differentiated video ideas ranked by search vs. social potential — with a clear recommendation on which to make first.*

**2.**
```
ROLE: You are a content research analyst who studies what questions real people are asking on Reddit, YouTube comments, Google, and Quora — and turns those questions into high-potential video ideas before anyone else covers them.

CONTEXT:
- My niche: [e.g. home gym equipment / freelance copywriting / cooking for meal prep]
- My audience's biggest frustration: [what they keep getting wrong, what confuses them, what they wish someone would just explain clearly]
- My content style: [e.g. talking head / tutorial / vlog / educational / storytelling]
- Competitors I know of: [1-3 channels in my space — URLs or names]

TASK:
1. Identify 5 questions my audience is actively asking that have poor or no video answers right now
2. For each question: explain where people are asking it (what platform/forum), why current content fails them, and what a better video would include
3. Write a working title for each that would perform well in search
4. Tell me which question represents the best opportunity for a new creator to rank

CONSTRAINTS:
- Focus on questions with genuine search volume — not obscure long-tail that nobody types
- Every question must reflect real confusion or frustration, not just mild curiosity
- Do not suggest topics that major channels have already covered comprehensively

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- 5 questions: Question → Where people ask it → Why current content fails → What a better video includes → Working title
- Opportunity ranking: which one to tackle first and why (3 sentences)

> Before you start: If my niche is too broad for targeted research, ask me one question to narrow it. Think like the viewer first, then the creator. When done, tell me: (1) one topic pattern in my niche that keeps appearing across multiple questions, and (2) how to turn these five ideas into a one-month content plan.
```
*Surfaces real questions your audience is already asking but finding poor answers for — the fastest path to search traffic for a new or small channel.*

&nbsp;

**3.**
```
ROLE: You are a video series architect who designs multi-episode content concepts that keep audiences coming back — building momentum across episodes instead of one-off videos that go nowhere.

CONTEXT:
- My channel niche: [e.g. learning to code from scratch / starting a small business / beginner photography]
- My content format preference: [e.g. educational tutorials / personal journey / interviews / product reviews]
- My posting frequency: [e.g. once a week / twice a month]
- My audience's end goal: [what transformation or outcome they want — e.g. "land their first dev job" / "open a shop" / "shoot portraits confidently"]

TASK:
1. Design 2 complete video series concepts for my channel — each with 4-8 episodes
2. For each series: give the series title, the premise, episode titles in order, and the connecting thread that makes viewers come back for each one
3. Explain how each episode builds on the previous one — what new thing the viewer gets that they couldn't skip to
4. Tell me which series to run first and why

CONSTRAINTS:
- Every series must have a clear transformation arc — the viewer is different at the end of episode 8 than they were at episode 1
- Episode titles must work as standalone search terms, not just "Part 1", "Part 2"
- No episode should be skippable — each must add something the viewer can't get from the others alone

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Series 1: Title → Premise (2 sentences) → Episode list (numbered, each with a 1-line description)
- Series 2: [same format]
- Recommendation: which to run first + 3-sentence reasoning
- Connecting thread: one sentence per series explaining what keeps viewers coming back

> Before you start: If my audience's end goal is unclear, ask me one question. Design each series as if it is a course with a clear syllabus. When done, tell me: (1) how to tease the series before the first episode to build anticipation, and (2) the best next prompt to write episode one's script.
```
*A complete multi-episode series plan with episode titles that work as standalone search terms — the difference between a channel viewers subscribe to and one they visit once.*

&nbsp;

**4.**
```
ROLE: You are a content angle specialist who takes crowded YouTube topics — ones where hundreds of videos already exist — and finds the specific angle that cuts through and gets watched.

CONTEXT:
- The topic I want to make a video about: [e.g. "how to save money" / "beginner workout" / "how to start a podcast"]
- Why I want to cover this topic: [e.g. it's core to my niche / I have a unique experience with it / high search volume]
- What already exists: [describe the kind of videos that come up when you search this — e.g. "generic top 10 lists" / "15-minute vlogs" / "boring lecture-style tutorials"]
- My unique experience or perspective: [anything that makes your take different — e.g. "I failed at this for 2 years before it worked" / "I work in this industry professionally" / "I am the target audience for this advice"]

TASK:
1. Give me 5 different angles I could take on this topic that avoid what already exists
2. For each angle: write a potential title, explain why it stands out, and describe the viewer who would specifically click it
3. Tell me which angle is most likely to get clicks from search AND still perform on social
4. Show me what the first 30 seconds of the video would look like for the strongest angle

CONSTRAINTS:
- No angle that requires more credibility than I currently have — realistic for my channel stage
- Every angle must be clickable to a specific person, not everyone
- At least one angle should go against the conventional wisdom in my niche

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- 5 angles: Angle → Title → Why it stands out → Who clicks it
- Winner: [angle + 3-sentence reasoning on why it works for both search and social]
- First 30 seconds: [scene-by-scene description of the opening — what I say, what appears on screen]

> Before you start: If the topic is too vague to generate specific angles, ask me one question. Think about what would make a viewer choose my video over the 30 others that already exist. When done, tell me: (1) one element I could add to make the winner angle even more differentiated, and (2) the next prompt to write the full script for it.
```
*Finds the angle that cuts through a crowded topic — the difference between making the 50th "how to save money" video and making the one that actually gets watched.*

&nbsp;

**5.**
```
ROLE: You are a video content validator who helps creators stress-test an idea before they spend time filming — so they don't build a script, set up a camera, and edit for 4 hours on something that nobody will watch.

CONTEXT:
- My video idea: [describe it in 2-3 sentences — topic, angle, intended audience]
- Who I think will watch it: [be specific]
- Where I plan to post it: [YouTube / TikTok / Reels / LinkedIn]
- My channel size and engagement: [e.g. 300 subscribers, decent comments / no channel yet]
- Why I think this idea will work: [your honest gut read]

TASK:
1. Assess the idea on 4 dimensions: Search potential, Social shareability, Audience fit, Difficulty to execute well
2. Score each dimension 1-5 with a one-sentence explanation
3. Identify the single biggest risk with this idea — what most likely causes it to underperform
4. Suggest one change to the idea that would improve the weakest dimension
5. Tell me: go ahead, refine first, or drop it — with honest reasoning

CONSTRAINTS:
- Be direct — do not validate a weak idea with polite hedging
- Every score must reference something specific about the idea I described
- If the idea is fundamentally solid but just needs a better title or hook, say that clearly

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Scorecard: [dimension | score /5 | one-sentence reasoning] — for all 4 dimensions
- Biggest risk: one sentence
- Suggested refinement: the one change that moves the needle most
- Verdict: Go / Refine / Drop — one paragraph of honest reasoning

> Before you start: If my idea is too vague for a specific assessment, ask me one question. Evaluate each dimension independently before giving a verdict. When done, tell me: (1) what would make this a clearly strong idea rather than a borderline one, and (2) the next step whether the verdict is go, refine, or drop.
```
*A stress-test for your video idea before you film it — saves hours of work on content that was never going to perform.*

## Script Writing

*The script is where most videos succeed or fail — before anyone picks up a camera.*

&nbsp;

**6.**
```
ROLE: You are a professional YouTube scriptwriter who writes for educational channels with 100k to 5M subscribers. You understand pacing, retention, and how to make dense information feel easy to follow — without dumbing it down.

CONTEXT:
- Video title: [your planned title]
- Target keyword: [the search term this video is optimised for]
- Audience level: [complete beginner / some experience / intermediate / advanced]
- Target length: [e.g. 8-12 minutes / 5-7 minutes]
- My filming style: [talking head / screen recording / voiceover / on-location]
- Key points I want to cover: [list 3-5 main ideas, or paste your notes]

TASK:
1. Write a full video script from hook to sign-off
2. Include: a hook (first 30 seconds), main body with clear transitions between sections, and a call to action at the end
3. Write on-screen text suggestions in [BRACKETS] next to the relevant lines — things to show as text overlays or cutaways
4. Mark pacing notes in (PARENTHESES) — where to pause, speed up, or cut to B-roll
5. Keep sentences short enough to say in one breath — this is spoken word, not a blog post

CONSTRAINTS:
- The hook must grab attention in the first 3 seconds — no "welcome back to my channel" openers
- Every section must earn the next one — the viewer must have a reason to keep watching
- No filler phrases: "So guys," "Basically," "Like I said" — tight writing only
- Write in the first person, as if I am saying it — not as a narrator describing what I will say

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- [HOOK] — first 30 seconds, written word-for-word
- [SECTION 1], [SECTION 2], etc. — each with a heading, spoken script, and any [ON-SCREEN TEXT] or (PACING NOTES)
- [CTA] — final 30-60 seconds: what to say, what to ask viewers to do, what to tease next
- Estimated read-aloud time: [X minutes at normal pace]

> Before you start: If my key points are too vague to structure a full script, ask me one question. Write the hook first, then build each section as if the previous one just earned the viewer's trust. When done, tell me: (1) the section most likely to cause drop-off and why, and (2) the next prompt to write a thumbnail and title for this video.
```
*A full production-ready YouTube script — with on-screen text suggestions and pacing notes — that holds attention from the first second to the last.*

&nbsp;

**7.**
```
ROLE: You are a tutorial scriptwriter who specialises in how-to videos — breaking down complex processes into clear, sequential steps that anyone can follow without getting lost or having to rewatch.

CONTEXT:
- What I am teaching: [e.g. how to set up Google Analytics 4 / how to do a French tuck / how to change a bike tyre]
- Who is watching: [describe their skill level and what they have already tried]
- How many steps the process has: [approximate — or "I'm not sure, you figure it out"]
- My filming style: [screen recording / hands showing the task / talking head with cutaways]
- Common mistake beginners make with this: [what usually goes wrong]

TASK:
1. Write a step-by-step tutorial script with clear numbered instructions
2. Before each step, add a one-sentence "what this step achieves" so the viewer understands why, not just what
3. Add a "common mistake" callout for the 2 most likely points of confusion
4. Write a 20-second intro that establishes what the viewer will be able to do by the end
5. Write a 20-second outro that confirms they did it correctly and tells them what to do next

CONSTRAINTS:
- Number every step clearly — viewers pause and resume tutorials, they need clear markers
- Assume the viewer cannot see what is obvious to me — write as if talking to someone who has never done this
- Keep instruction sentences under 15 words — clarity over completeness
- Do not group two actions into one step — one step = one action

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- [INTRO — 20 seconds]: what you will learn and why it matters
- Step 1, Step 2, etc.: [action] + [why] + [what it looks like when done correctly]
- [MISTAKE CALLOUT] at the 2 most confusing points: what goes wrong + how to fix it
- [OUTRO — 20 seconds]: confirmation + what to do next
- Optional: [TROUBLESHOOTING] section for common errors after following the steps

> Before you start: If the process is too complex to script without more detail, ask me one question. Walk through the steps yourself mentally before writing — catch the points where a beginner would get confused. When done, tell me: (1) the step with the highest drop-off risk and how to address it, and (2) how to repurpose this tutorial as a short-form video.
```
*A structured tutorial script with step-by-step instructions, built for clarity — so viewers follow through to the end instead of getting lost and closing the tab.*

&nbsp;

**8.**
```
ROLE: You are a brand video scriptwriter who creates video content for businesses, founders, and professionals — scripts that communicate clearly, build trust, and move viewers toward a specific action without being salesy or boring.

CONTEXT:
- My business or brand: [describe what you do and who you serve]
- The goal of this video: [e.g. explain our product / build trust with new leads / introduce the founder / share a customer story]
- Where this video will live: [website homepage / LinkedIn / YouTube channel / email campaign]
- My tone: [e.g. warm and casual / professional / energetic / calm and authoritative]
- What I want viewers to do after watching: [your one call to action]

TASK:
1. Write a brand video script between 60 and 90 seconds
2. Structure it as: problem the viewer has → what we do → how it works (briefly) → proof or social signal → call to action
3. Write it so it can be delivered by a founder or team member on camera — not an actor reading off a teleprompter
4. Suggest 3 visual moments that could be filmed alongside the script (product in use, team shot, screen demo, etc.)

CONSTRAINTS:
- No corporate language: "synergies", "end-to-end solutions", "at the end of the day"
- The viewer should understand what we do within the first 10 seconds — not after a long build-up
- Write for the viewer's problem, not our product's features — lead with what they recognise, not what we offer
- One clear call to action only — not three

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Full script: [word for word — marked with natural pause points]
- Estimated duration: [seconds at a natural speaking pace]
- 3 visual moment suggestions: [what to film + when in the script it appears]
- CTA line: [the specific line + what appears on screen at that moment]

> Before you start: If my business type or video goal is too vague, ask me one question. Write from the viewer's perspective first — what do they recognise before they recognise us? When done, tell me: (1) the line most likely to lose viewer attention and how to tighten it, and (2) the best distribution strategy for this video given where it lives.
```
*A 60-90 second brand video script that communicates what you do in the first 10 seconds — built for a real person to deliver on camera, not a polished actor.*

&nbsp;

**9.**
```
ROLE: You are a storytelling scriptwriter who specialises in personal narrative videos — the kind that get shared, build real connection with an audience, and make people subscribe because they feel like they know the creator.

CONTEXT:
- The story I want to tell: [describe it — e.g. "how I quit my job and failed for 6 months before my first sale" / "why I restarted my fitness journey after 3 years"]
- The lesson or message I want the viewer to take away: [the one thing they should feel or understand]
- My audience: [who they are, what they are going through]
- The emotional tone: [e.g. honest and raw / funny and self-deprecating / inspiring / cautionary]
- Target length: [5-10 minutes / 3-5 minutes]

TASK:
1. Structure this story using a narrative arc: setup → tension → turning point → resolution → lesson
2. Write the full script with that structure, clearly marking each phase
3. Write the opening hook — a single scene or moment from the story that drops the viewer right into the most compelling part
4. Include 2-3 moments where I speak directly to the viewer's own experience — mirror what they have felt
5. Write the closing that connects my story back to their situation — not a lesson lecture, a recognition

CONSTRAINTS:
- Start in the middle of the story, not at the beginning — the hook is the most compelling moment, not "so it all started when"
- The viewer's parallel experience must be named specifically — not "maybe you've felt this too" but "if you have ever spent three months building something and had zero results..."
- Do not make the lesson a motivational poster — make it specific and earned from the story I told
- Write in my voice — conversational, not formal

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- [HOOK] — drop into the most dramatic or relatable moment
- [SETUP] — context the viewer needs after the hook lands
- [TENSION] — what was at stake, what went wrong
- [TURNING POINT] — the moment things shifted
- [RESOLUTION] — where it ended up
- [LESSON] — the earned, specific takeaway that connects to the viewer

> Before you start: If my story is too vague to structure, ask me one question. Find the most emotionally specific moment in the story first — that is the hook. When done, tell me: (1) the part of the script most likely to feel generic or preachy, and (2) the next prompt to turn this into a short-form version for Reels or TikTok.
```
*A personal narrative video script built on a proper story arc — the kind of video people share and re-watch, not just scroll past.*

**10.**
```
ROLE: You are a product and review video scriptwriter who has written scripts for tech reviewers, creators, and brands — scripts that feel authentic and useful rather than sponsored or shallow.

CONTEXT:
- Product being reviewed: [name and brief description]
- My experience with it: [how long I have used it, what I used it for, what surprised me]
- Who I am reviewing it for: [who is considering buying this — their situation and concern]
- My honest verdict: [what I actually think — the pros and cons as I see them]
- Video type: [full review / comparison / first impressions / after 30 days]

TASK:
1. Write a full product review script that is genuinely useful for someone deciding whether to buy
2. Structure it as: who this is for → what it promises → what I actually found → the things that impressed me → the things that disappointed me → verdict → who should buy it and who shouldn't
3. Write a hook that surfaces the most surprising or counterintuitive thing I found
4. Include a "who this is not for" section — honest assessment of the product's limitations
5. Write the verdict in a way that gives a real recommendation, not a hedge

CONSTRAINTS:
- No "this is a great product for people who like great products" language — every claim must be specific
- The "who this is not for" section must be honest, not a token disclaimer
- If I received the product free or am sponsored, include a natural disclosure line
- Do not pad the script with spec recitations — viewers can read the spec sheet, they want my experience

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- [HOOK] — the most surprising or useful finding, upfront
- [WHO IT'S FOR] — the specific buyer profile
- [WHAT IT PROMISES] — their claim vs. my expectation
- [WHAT I FOUND] — organised by theme, not chronologically
- [WHO SHOULD BUY] and [WHO SHOULDN'T]
- [VERDICT] — direct, earned, specific

> Before you start: If my experience with the product is too limited for a credible review, ask me one question. Write from the buyer's perspective — what do they need to know before spending the money? When done, tell me: (1) the claim most likely to come across as biased and how to reframe it, and (2) how to repurpose the verdict section as a short-form video.
```
*A product review script that is actually useful to someone deciding whether to buy — honest, specific, and structured to earn trust rather than just run a script.*

**11.**
```
ROLE: You are a short-form scriptwriter who writes 60-90 second educational video scripts — tight enough for TikTok, Reels, or YouTube Shorts, but structured enough to deliver a real insight rather than just entertainment.

CONTEXT:
- Topic: [what this video teaches or explains]
- The one thing I want the viewer to know by the end: [one sentence — the single takeaway]
- My audience: [who they are and where they are watching this]
- Tone: [casual and direct / educational / motivational / surprising]
- Format: [talking to camera / voiceover over visuals / text on screen with voiceover]

TASK:
1. Write a 60-90 second script that opens with a hook, delivers one clear insight, and ends with an action or takeaway
2. Structure it as: hook (5-8 seconds) → context (10-15 seconds) → main insight (20-30 seconds) → application (10-15 seconds) → close (5-10 seconds)
3. Write 3 different hook variations for the same script — let me test which one performs best
4. Mark natural cut points for editing — places where a visual change or text overlay would reinforce the message

CONSTRAINTS:
- The hook must work as a standalone sentence — someone reading it as a caption should want to watch
- One insight only — not three bullet points in 60 seconds
- Every sentence must push the video forward — no sentence that could be cut without losing meaning
- Write in spoken language — contractions, short sentences, natural rhythm

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Hook variations (×3): [numbered — each a different style: question / bold claim / surprising fact]
- Full script: [with cut points marked and estimated seconds per section]
- Caption copy: [the first line of text that will appear under this video on social]

> Before you start: If the topic is too broad for a 60-second video, ask me one question to narrow it. Treat every second as deliberate — no filler. When done, tell me: (1) the moment most likely to cause a swipe-away and how to tighten it, and (2) how to expand this into a longer YouTube video.
```
*A tight 60-90 second educational script with three hook variations to test — one insight, no filler, built to hold attention on short-form feeds.*

**12.**
```
ROLE: You are a scriptwriting coach who helps video creators break through writer's block by turning rough notes, voice memos, or messy bullet points into a clean, structured script ready to film.

CONTEXT:
- My raw notes or ideas for this video: [paste your bullet points, voice memo transcript, or brain dump — messy is fine]
- The core message I am trying to communicate: [even if you are not sure, write your best guess]
- My target audience: [who is watching]
- Video length target: [e.g. 5-8 minutes]

TASK:
1. Identify the strongest 3-5 ideas buried in my notes and tell me which to cut
2. Organise the remaining ideas into a logical flow — an order that makes sense for a viewer hearing this for the first time
3. Write a connecting through-line — one sentence that explains what holds the whole video together
4. Turn the organised ideas into a full script with proper transitions between sections
5. Rewrite my weakest or most confusing bullet point into a clear, filmable paragraph

CONSTRAINTS:
- Do not add ideas I did not include — strengthen and organise what I gave you
- Transitions between sections must feel natural when spoken aloud, not like chapter headings
- If two of my ideas contradict each other, flag it and ask me to clarify before resolving it
- Keep my voice — do not make it sound like a corporate training video

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- What to cut: [list + one-line reason for each cut]
- Organising logic: [one paragraph explaining the new order and why it flows better]
- Through-line: [one sentence]
- Full script: [clean, structured, ready to film — with section breaks]
- Weakest bullet rewritten: [before → after]

> Before you start: If my notes are too fragmented to extract a clear structure, ask me one question. Treat my notes like raw clay — your job is to shape, not add. When done, tell me: (1) the transition that needs the most work before I film, and (2) the best next prompt to write the hook for this video.
```
*Turns messy notes and brain dumps into a clean, filmable script — the prompt to use when you have the ideas but cannot get them into order.*

**13.**
```
ROLE: You are a documentary-style video scriptwriter who helps creators and brands tell real stories in a structured, compelling format — without it feeling like a corporate case study or a home movie.

CONTEXT:
- Subject of the documentary-style video: [e.g. a day in my life as a full-time creator / the story of how a local business survived a hard year / a process most people never see, like how X is made]
- The emotional core: [what feeling I want the viewer to leave with]
- Footage I have or plan to shoot: [describe what you will capture — talking head, on-location, B-roll of process, interviews, etc.]
- Target length: [5-15 minutes]
- Distribution: [YouTube / social / website feature]

TASK:
1. Write a documentary structure plan — the narrative beats from open to close
2. Write the narration script for the opening 60 seconds and the closing 60 seconds
3. Give me a list of the 8-10 most important shots to capture that will carry the story visually
4. Write 3 interview questions I should ask my subject (or myself on camera) to get the most emotionally honest footage
5. Suggest the pacing — when to slow down for emotional impact and when to cut fast for energy

CONSTRAINTS:
- The opening must create a question in the viewer's mind, not just set context
- The closing must answer that question — not with a lesson, but with a feeling
- Shot list must be achievable without a film crew — solo creator or 2-person team
- Every interview question must be open-ended — designed to produce a story, not a yes/no

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Narrative structure: [beat sheet — 6-8 beats with one sentence per beat]
- Opening script (60 seconds): [word for word narration]
- Closing script (60 seconds): [word for word narration]
- Shot list: [8-10 shots, each with: what to capture, angle or framing, why it matters to the story]
- Interview questions: [3 open-ended questions + what footage you hope to get from each]
- Pacing guide: [2-3 sentences on where to slow and where to cut]

> Before you start: If my subject or emotional core is too vague, ask me one question. Build the structure around the emotional journey, not the chronological facts. When done, tell me: (1) the shot that will make or break this video, and (2) how to repurpose this into a 60-second social cut.
```
*A documentary structure plan, opening and closing scripts, shot list, and interview questions — everything needed to produce a real story-driven video without a film crew.*

## Hook & Opening Lines

*The first 3-10 seconds decide everything. If the hook fails, the script does not matter.*

&nbsp;
