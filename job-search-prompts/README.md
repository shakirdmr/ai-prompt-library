# Job Search Prompts — 50 Free Copy-Paste Examples

## What is this?

This is a collection of prompts for every stage of finding a job — from figuring out what roles to apply for, to cold messaging recruiters, to acing your interview, to evaluating an offer.

Most people search for jobs passively: scroll job boards, send the same resume everywhere, and wait. AI lets you be proactive — writing cold messages, preparing for specific interviews, and practicing answers — all in minutes. These prompts are written to work even if you have never used AI before.

## The 5-Part Prompt Structure

Every prompt here uses a professional framework used by experienced AI practitioners:

| Part | What it does |
|---|---|
| **ROLE** | Tells the AI who to be — an expert, not a generalist |
| **CONTEXT** | Gives the AI the specific details it needs about your situation |
| **TASK** | Tells the AI exactly what to produce |
| **CONSTRAINTS** | Sets the rules — tone, length, what to avoid |
| **OUTPUT FORMAT** | Specifies exactly how the answer should be structured |

**Plus a power footer** — each prompt ends with an instruction that triggers the AI to ask clarifying questions, think step by step, and suggest what to do next. This is the difference between an average AI user and a power user.

## How to use this

1. Open [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com), [Claude](https://claude.ai), or [Gemini](https://gemini.google.com) — all free to use
2. Find the section that matches where you are in your job search
3. Copy the full prompt from the code block
4. Replace everything inside `[brackets]` with your real information
5. Paste it into the AI and hit send

> **Tip for beginners:** Start with the "Finding the Right Role" section if you are unsure what jobs to apply for — it is the most overlooked step. Most people jump straight to applying without first being clear on what they actually want or what roles match their skills.

## Finding the Right Role

*Use these before you start applying — clarity on what to search for will save you weeks of wasted applications.*

&nbsp;

**1.**
```
ROLE: You are a career strategist and labor market expert with 15 years of experience helping professionals identify overlooked job titles that match their skill set.

CONTEXT:
- My current job title: [e.g. Social Media Manager]
- Years of experience: [X]
- Core skills: [e.g. content creation, analytics, copywriting, campaign management]
- Industries I have worked in: [e.g. e-commerce, SaaS]
- Location or work preference: [city / remote / hybrid]

TASK:
1. Generate 10 job title variations I should search for, including ones I might not have considered
2. For each title, explain why my background is a match and what skills transfer directly
3. Flag 2-3 titles that are growing in demand right now

CONSTRAINTS:
- Be specific to my background — no generic advice
- Include both lateral moves and slight upward moves
- Avoid titles that require qualifications I don't have

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Numbered list: Title → Why I qualify → Demand outlook (growing / stable / declining)
- End with a suggested job board search string I can copy directly

> Before you start: If anything in my context is missing or unclear, ask me one specific question first. Then work through your answer step by step. When done, tell me: (1) one piece of information that would make your answer even stronger, and (2) the best next prompt I should run after this one.
```
*Surfaces job titles you didn't know you were qualified for — a social media manager often qualifies for content strategist, digital marketing coordinator, or brand communications manager roles without realising it.*

*People who are putting in effort but not getting responses — often a resume, outreach, or targeting problem. This prompt treats your job search like a broken funnel and diagnoses exactly where it is leaking.*

## Cold Outreach to Recruiters & Hiring Managers

*Use these to get noticed before a role is even posted — most jobs are filled through outreach and referrals, not job boards.*

&nbsp;

**6.**
```
ROLE: You are a recruiter who has read thousands of LinkedIn cold messages and knows exactly what gets ignored versus what gets a reply.

CONTEXT:
- Target company: [e.g. Stripe]
- Role I am targeting: [e.g. Growth Marketing Manager]
- Recruiter's name and role: [if known]
- One specific thing about the company I can reference: [e.g. their recent expansion into APAC]
- My strongest qualification for this role: [e.g. scaled growth from $0 to $2M ARR at a startup]

TASK:
1. Write a LinkedIn connection request message under 300 characters
2. Write a follow-up InMail message under 150 words if they accept but don't reply
3. Explain what makes each version effective

CONSTRAINTS:
- The connection request must not feel like a job ask — it should spark curiosity
- No flattery, no "I'm a big fan" — only specific, relevant observations
- The follow-up should be direct and have a clear single ask

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Connection request: [message — must be under 300 characters]
- Follow-up InMail: [message — under 150 words]
- Why this works: [2-3 sentences per message]

> Before you start: If anything in my context is missing or unclear, ask me one specific question first. Then work through your answer step by step. When done, tell me: (1) one piece of information that would make your answer even stronger, and (2) the best next prompt I should run after this one.
```
*LinkedIn connection requests need to be short — long messages get ignored. This produces a two-step outreach sequence: a curiosity-triggering connection request, then a clear follow-up.*

## Interview Preparation

*Use these to prepare for specific interviews so you walk in confident instead of winging it.*

&nbsp;

**11.**
```
ROLE: You are an executive interview coach who has prepped candidates for roles at Google, McKinsey, and top-tier startups using the STAR method.

CONTEXT:
- Role I am interviewing for: [e.g. Product Manager]
- Company: [e.g. Atlassian]
- My resume or work summary: [paste a brief summary or key bullet points]
- Interview format: [e.g. two 45-minute rounds — one behavioural, one case study]
- My biggest concern going in: [e.g. I don't have direct PM experience]

TASK:
1. Give me the 10 most likely interview questions for this specific role at this company
2. Write a strong STAR-method answer for each question based on my background
3. Flag the 2 questions I should prepare most carefully and explain why

CONSTRAINTS:
- Tailor each question and answer to the specific company, not just the role
- STAR answers must use concrete examples, not hypothetical ones — if you need real examples, ask me
- Flag where my answer might be weak and suggest how to strengthen it

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- For each question: Question → STAR answer → One coaching note (what to watch for when delivering this)
- End with: Top 2 questions to rehearse out loud before the interview

> Before you start: If anything in my context is missing or unclear, ask me one specific question first. Then work through your answer step by step. When done, tell me: (1) one piece of information that would make your answer even stronger, and (2) the best next prompt I should run after this one.
```
*This is the highest-ROI interview prep you can do. Give the AI your actual resume and the company name and it will generate answers tailored to your experience — not generic STAR templates.*

## Handling Difficult Interview Questions

*Use these to prepare honest, confident answers for the questions most people fumble.*

&nbsp;

**16.**
```
ROLE: You are a career coach who specialises in helping candidates frame difficult career moments — layoffs, firings, gaps — in a way that is honest, professional, and does not raise red flags.

CONTEXT:
- Why I left my last role: [e.g. laid off in a company-wide restructure / let go after a performance issue / quit without another job lined up]
- How long ago it happened: [e.g. 3 months ago]
- What I have been doing since: [e.g. freelancing, upskilling, caregiving, searching]
- The role I am interviewing for: [role and company]

TASK:
1. Write a confident, honest answer to "Why did you leave your last role?"
2. Write a version if the interviewer follows up with "Can you tell me more about that?"
3. Flag anything in my situation that could raise a red flag and suggest how to address it proactively

CONSTRAINTS:
- The answer must be honest — do not suggest I lie or omit facts that would come up in a reference check
- Keep the main answer under 60 seconds when spoken aloud
- Do not badmouth the previous employer — reframe all negatives in neutral or positive terms

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Main answer: [spoken version, under 60 seconds]
- Follow-up answer: [if they probe deeper — 2-3 sentences]
- Red flag audit: [what might concern them + how to pre-empt it]

> Before you start: If anything in my context is missing or unclear, ask me one specific question first. Then work through your answer step by step. When done, tell me: (1) one piece of information that would make your answer even stronger, and (2) the best next prompt I should run after this one.
```
*Layoffs are common — a calm, prepared answer removes any awkwardness immediately and signals professionalism. Improvising this answer is one of the most costly interview mistakes.*
