# Resume Writing Prompts — Structured for Expert-Level Results

## What is this?

A collection of structured prompts for building a complete, professional resume using **ChatGPT**, **Claude**, or **Gemini** — organised in the exact order you build one, top to bottom.

Every prompt follows a five-part structure that produces expert-level output even if you have never used AI before:

| Part | What it does |
|---|---|
| **ROLE** | Tells the AI who to be — a specific expert with real credentials |
| **CONTEXT** | Your situation — fill in the brackets with your real details |
| **TASK** | Numbered steps — exactly what the AI must do |
| **CONSTRAINTS** | Rules that keep the output focused and honest |
| **OUTPUT FORMAT** | Tells the AI exactly what the response should look like |

Every prompt also ends with a **power line** that triggers: asking for missing info before starting, step-by-step thinking, and a suggestion for what to do next.

## 1. Headshot & Profile Photo

*The photo at the top of your resume or LinkedIn profile is the first impression before anyone reads a word.*

```
ROLE: You are a professional headshot and personal branding photographer who has shot thousands of LinkedIn and resume photos. You know exactly what makes a headshot look credible, warm, and professional — and what makes one look amateurish.

CONTEXT:
- My job title / target role: [what you do or want to do]
- My industry: [e.g. finance, tech, healthcare, creative, law]
- Where I need this photo: [resume / LinkedIn / both]
- Country I am applying in: [important — photo norms vary by country]
- Camera I have access to: [smartphone / DSLR / neither — need AI generated]

TASK:
1. Tell me exactly what to wear for my industry and role — colour, style, formality
2. Describe the ideal background — colour, setting, how far away
3. Describe the lighting setup I can achieve at home without professional gear
4. Describe the exact expression and pose — where to look, how to position my shoulders
5. Tell me whether including a photo on my resume is expected or harmful in my target country — and why
6. If I have no camera: write a detailed Midjourney or DALL-E prompt to generate a professional AI headshot that matches my description

CONSTRAINTS:
- Be specific — not "look professional", but exactly what that means for my industry
- Country advice must be accurate: photos on resumes are expected in Germany, discouraged in the US and UK
- The AI image prompt must produce a realistic, photographic result — not illustrated

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Outfit: [specific description]
- Background: [specific description]
- Lighting: [step-by-step home setup]
- Expression and pose: [exact instructions]
- Photo on resume: [yes/no for my country + reason]
- AI image prompt (if needed): [full ready-to-paste prompt]

> Before you start: If my industry or country is unclear, ask me one question. Give advice specific to my situation, not general guidelines. When done, tell me the one thing most people get wrong with headshots — and how I avoid it.
```
*Covers everything from what to wear to generating an AI headshot — and tells you whether a photo on your resume helps or hurts in your specific country.*

## 2. Resume Header & Contact Info

*The top section of your resume — your name, title, and contact details. Often done wrong.*

```
ROLE: You are a professional resume writer and career coach who has reviewed over 5,000 resumes. You know exactly what a recruiter looks at in the first 6 seconds — and what makes them stop or continue.

CONTEXT:
- My full name: [name]
- My current or target job title: [what you do or want to be known as]
- My email: [email address]
- My phone: [number]
- My LinkedIn: [URL or username]
- My portfolio / GitHub / personal site (if relevant): [URL or "none"]
- My location: [city and country — do not include full street address]
- Role I am applying for: [target job title and industry]

TASK:
1. Format my complete resume header — clean, modern, professional
2. Rewrite my professional title line to be specific, keyword-rich, and relevant to my target role — not just my current job title
3. Tell me exactly which contact details to include and which to remove — with a reason for each
4. Tell me whether to include my full address or just city/country for my target market
5. If my LinkedIn URL is unclean (random numbers), tell me how to customise it

CONSTRAINTS:
- No full street address — city and country only
- Professional title must differ from a generic job title — it should be a value statement
- Every contact detail included must serve a purpose for the recruiter — cut anything decorative

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Formatted header block (copy-paste ready)
- Professional title: [current] → [rewritten with reason]
- Contact details: table — [item] | [include?] | [reason]
- Address format recommendation: [city/country only or more — with reason]
- LinkedIn URL tip (if applicable)

> Before you start: If my target role differs significantly from my current title, ask me one question about what I want to be positioned as. When done, tell me the most common header mistake you see — and check if I'm making it.
```
*Builds your resume header correctly and rewrites your professional title into a keyword-rich value statement.*

## 3. Professional Summary

*The 3–5 lines just below your header — the most-read section on any resume.*

```
ROLE: You are a senior resume writer and executive career coach who writes professional summaries for clients at all levels — from fresh graduates to C-suite executives. Your summaries pass ATS filters, hook recruiters in 6 seconds, and position the candidate for exactly the role they want.

CONTEXT:
- My current job title: [title]
- My target role: [what I am applying for]
- Years of experience: [number]
- My top 2–3 skills or areas of expertise: [list them]
- My most impressive achievement (with a number if possible): [e.g. "grew revenue by 40%" or "managed a team of 12"]
- Career situation: [standard progression / career change / re-entering after a gap / first job]
- Company type I am targeting: [startup / enterprise / agency / non-profit / any]

TASK:
1. Write 3 versions of my professional summary (3–4 sentences each)
2. Version A: Optimised for ATS keyword matching
3. Version B: Optimised for human reader engagement — more voice, less keyword stuffing
4. Version C: Tailored for my specific career situation (career change / gap / new grad / etc.)
5. After the 3 versions, recommend which to use and explain why in 2 sentences

CONSTRAINTS:
- No clichés: "results-driven", "passionate", "dynamic", "seasoned professional", "go-getter"
- Every sentence must earn its place — no filler
- Each version must feel different — not just word swaps
- ATS version must include my target job title and 2–3 skills naturally in the text

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Version A (ATS): [text]
- Version B (Human): [text]
- Version C (Situation-specific): [text]
- Recommendation: [A/B/C] — [2-sentence reason]

> Before you start: If my career situation is unclear, ask me one question. Write each version as if a recruiter has 6 seconds. When done, tell me: (1) the word or phrase that weakens summaries most, and (2) how to customise the recommended version for a specific company.
```
*3 ready-to-use summary versions — ATS-optimised, human-readable, and situation-specific.*

## 4. Work Experience & Bullet Points

*The main body of your resume — where most hiring decisions are made.*

```
ROLE: You are a professional resume writer who specialises in transforming lists of job duties into achievement-focused, quantified resume bullets that make candidates stand out. You write for ATS systems and human readers simultaneously.

CONTEXT:
- Job title: [your title at this role]
- Company: [company name and brief description if not well known]
- Dates: [start month/year — end month/year or "present"]
- My responsibilities and tasks: [list everything you did — bullet points or sentences, messy is fine]
- My achievements (with numbers if you have them): [what improved, grew, saved, or changed because of you]
- Target role I am applying for: [the job I am now seeking]

TASK:
1. Write 5–6 achievement-focused bullet points for this role using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result)
2. Lead each bullet with a different strong action verb
3. Quantify every bullet where possible — use my numbers or reasonably infer realistic ones
4. Ensure at least 3 bullets contain a keyword relevant to my target role
5. Write one optional "stretch bullet" — a bullet that makes this role sound as impressive as possible while remaining fully truthful

CONSTRAINTS:
- Never start two bullets with the same verb
- No "responsible for" or "helped with" — only direct ownership language
- If I have no exact numbers, use ranges or qualitative scale (e.g. "significantly reduced", "cross-functional team of 8+")
- Every bullet must answer: "So what? Why does this matter to my next employer?"

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- 5 core bullets (numbered)
- 1 stretch bullet (labelled)
- After bullets: a one-line note on which keywords were included and why

> Before you start: If my responsibilities list is too vague to write specific bullets, ask me one question. Build from achievement down to task — not the other way around. When done, tell me: (1) the weakest bullet and how to strengthen it, and (2) how many bullets are appropriate for a role of this seniority.
```
*Transforms any list of job duties into strong, quantified, ATS-ready resume bullets.*

## 5. Skills Section

*A structured list of what you know — not a random wall of keywords.*

```
ROLE: You are a resume strategist and ATS optimisation specialist. You know which skills get resumes through automated filters, which ones impress humans, and which ones actually hurt a candidate by signalling they don't know what's relevant.

CONTEXT:
- My job target: [role and industry]
- Everything I know how to do (messy list is fine): [tools, software, languages, methodologies, certifications, soft skills — everything]
- The job description I am applying for (optional but powerful): [paste JD or key phrases]
- My experience level: [junior / mid / senior / executive]

TASK:
1. Select the skills from my list that are most relevant to my target role — remove anything too basic or irrelevant
2. Organise them into 3–4 clean categories (e.g. Technical Skills, Tools & Software, Languages, Core Competencies)
3. Add any skills I likely have but forgot to mention — based on my role and experience level
4. Flag any skills that could actually hurt me (e.g. outdated tech, things that signal junior level for a senior role)
5. Format the section for a modern one-column or two-column resume layout

CONSTRAINTS:
- Cut anything every candidate lists — "Microsoft Word", "email", "internet"
- Every skill included must be something a recruiter for this role would look for
- Do not invent skills I didn't indicate I have — only suggest ones I may have overlooked
- Soft skills must be specific — "communication" alone is weak; "cross-functional stakeholder communication" is strong

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Skills section (copy-paste ready, formatted)
- Removed skills: [list] — [one-line reason for each removal]
- Suggested additions: [list] — [one-line reason for each suggestion]
- One formatting note for my resume layout

> Before you start: If my skills list or target role is too vague, ask me one question. Curate aggressively — a tight skills section is stronger than a long one. When done, tell me the one skill category most candidates in my field forget to include.
```
*Turns a messy skills dump into a curated, ATS-optimised section — and flags skills that hurt rather than help.*

## 6. Education Section

*Degrees, certifications, and training — formatted correctly for your experience level.*

```
ROLE: You are a professional resume writer who knows exactly how to present educational credentials at every career stage — from new graduates leading with their degree to senior professionals who should bury it after their experience.

CONTEXT:
- Degree(s): [degree name, field of study, university, graduation year]
- GPA (if you want to include it): [number, or "prefer not to include"]
- Relevant coursework (if applicable): [list 3–5 courses relevant to your target role]
- Certifications: [name, issuing body, year — list all]
- Online courses or bootcamps: [name, platform, year — list all]
- My experience level: [student / 0–2 years / 3–7 years / 8+ years]
- My target role: [what I am applying for]

TASK:
1. Write my complete education section — formatted correctly for my experience level
2. Tell me whether education should appear before or after work experience on my resume — and why, for my specific situation
3. Advise on GPA inclusion — should I include it? Why or why not?
4. Format my certifications separately from my degree if appropriate
5. If I have non-traditional education (bootcamp, online courses, self-taught) — write it in a way that presents it credibly alongside or instead of a formal degree

CONSTRAINTS:
- If I have 5+ years of experience, education should be concise — one line per entry, no coursework
- GPA should only be included if it is above 3.5 (US) or a first/2:1 (UK) and I am within 3 years of graduating
- Online courses from credible platforms (Coursera, Google, AWS, etc.) are worth including — random YouTube courses are not
- No high school education unless I have no degree at all

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Education section (copy-paste ready)
- Placement recommendation: before or after experience — [reason]
- GPA decision: include / exclude — [reason]
- One note on how to handle my specific situation (new grad / career changer / non-traditional education)

> Before you start: If I have no formal degree but want to apply for a degree-required role, ask me one question before advising. Format for my career stage, not a template. When done, tell me the one education section mistake that makes experienced candidates look junior.
```
*Correctly formats your education for your career stage — and tells you whether it helps or hurts to put it near the top.*

## 7. Projects Section

*Especially important for students, career changers, developers, and designers.*

```
ROLE: You are a technical resume writer and career coach who specialises in helping people showcase side projects, portfolio work, and non-job experience in ways that impress hiring managers and demonstrate real capability.

CONTEXT:
- My target role: [what I am applying for]
- My projects (describe each briefly):
  Project 1: [name, what it does, tools/tech used, any results or users]
  Project 2: [name, what it does, tools/tech used, any results or users]
  Project 3: [name, what it does, tools/tech used, any results — add more if needed]
- Links (if available): [GitHub, live URL, portfolio, Behance, etc.]
- Why I am including projects: [no work experience yet / career change / want to show specific skills / all three]

TASK:
1. Select the 2–3 best projects to feature based on my target role — tell me which to cut and why
2. Write a 2–3 line description for each selected project: what it is, what it demonstrates, and any measurable outcome
3. Format the section with project name, tech/tools used, dates (if relevant), and link
4. Tell me how to frame a personal or university project so it reads as seriously as work experience
5. If I have no projects yet — suggest 2–3 projects I could build in 2–4 weeks that would directly impress a recruiter for my target role

CONSTRAINTS:
- Every project description must answer: "What skill does this prove, and why should the recruiter care?"
- Do not describe what the project IS in detail — describe what building it DEMONSTRATES
- School assignments count if they are substantial — but must be framed as professional work, not homework
- If a project is incomplete, tell me whether to include it and how to frame it honestly

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Recommended projects: [which to include] — [which to cut + reason]
- Project entries (copy-paste ready, formatted):
  [Project Name] | [Tech/Tools] | [Link if available]
  [2–3 line description]
- Framing tip: one sentence on how to make personal projects sound professional
- New project suggestions (if needed): 2–3 ideas with: project name + skills it demonstrates + estimated build time

> Before you start: If my project descriptions are too vague to write strong entries, ask me one specific question. Focus on what the project proves, not what it does. When done, tell me: (1) the most impressive project type for my specific target role, and (2) how to talk about a project in an interview if the interviewer asks me to walk through it.
```
*Writes project descriptions that demonstrate real capability — and suggests what to build if you have nothing yet.*

## 8. Handling Gaps & Tricky Situations

*Employment gaps, career changes, layoffs, freelance history, and non-linear paths.*

```
ROLE: You are a career coach and resume strategist who has helped thousands of candidates navigate difficult resume situations — gaps, layoffs, career changes, long tenures, and non-traditional paths. You are direct, honest, and know what actually works with modern recruiters.

CONTEXT:
- My situation: [describe your specific challenge — e.g. "2-year gap from 2022–2024 while caregiving", "was laid off in a mass redundancy", "freelanced for 3 years", "promoted twice at the same company for 8 years"]
- Role I am now applying for: [target job title and industry]
- How I want to be perceived: [e.g. "as someone who chose this gap intentionally" / "as someone who moved on professionally after a layoff"]
- Biggest concern: [what you are most worried a recruiter will think]

TASK:
1. Tell me directly — is my situation a real problem for recruiters, or am I overthinking it?
2. Write the exact language to use on my resume to address this situation honestly and positively
3. Write a cover letter paragraph that addresses it proactively — so the recruiter doesn't have to ask
4. Write a 30-second verbal answer I can use in an interview if asked about it
5. Tell me what NOT to say — the phrases or framings that make this situation sound worse than it is

CONSTRAINTS:
- Do not sugarcoat — tell me honestly if this situation will be a red flag and to what degree
- All language must be truthful — no fabrications or misleading framings
- Verbal answer must sound natural, not rehearsed
- Interview answer should be 30 seconds maximum — under-explaining is better than over-explaining

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Honest assessment: [is this a real problem? scale of 1–5 with reasoning]
- Resume language: [exact text to add, with context for where on the resume]
- Cover letter paragraph: [ready to paste]
- Verbal interview answer: [30-second script]
- What NOT to say: 3 bullet points

> Before you start: If my situation is complex or has multiple elements, ask me one question to clarify the most important part. Be direct — I can handle an honest answer. When done, tell me: (1) whether I should proactively address this or wait until asked, and (2) how most candidates make this situation worse than it needs to be.
```
*Direct, honest advice for navigating gaps, layoffs, and non-linear career paths — with exact language for resume, cover letter, and interview.*

## 9. Tailoring for a Specific Job

*Customising your resume for each application — the step most candidates skip.*

```
ROLE: You are an ATS optimisation specialist and recruiter who has reviewed over 10,000 resumes. You know exactly how applicant tracking systems score resumes — and what the human recruiter does in the 6 seconds after ATS approves it.

CONTEXT:
- My current resume summary: [paste your summary]
- My top 3 work experience bullets: [paste them]
- The job description I am applying for: [paste full JD]
- The company name: [company]
- One thing I know about this company's culture or mission (if any): [or "I'm not sure"]

TASK:
1. Extract the top 10 ATS keywords from this job description — ranked by importance
2. Identify which keywords are missing from my resume and must be added
3. Rewrite my summary to align with this specific role's requirements and language
4. Rewrite my top 3 bullets to match the JD's priorities — keeping achievements but adjusting language
5. Tell me the one thing about this company or role that I should reference in my cover letter to show I did my research

CONSTRAINTS:
- Every keyword added must be added naturally — not stuffed
- Do not change the facts of my experience — only the framing and language
- The summary rewrite must still sound like me — not a copy of the JD
- Flag if the role requires a qualification I do not have — do not hide this from me

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Top 10 ATS keywords: numbered list with [keyword] — [importance: high/medium]
- Missing from my resume: [keywords] — [where to add each]
- Rewritten summary: [text]
- Rewritten bullets: [numbered, with before/after for each]
- Company research note: [one specific thing to mention]

> Before you start: If the JD is missing key information or is very generic, ask me one question. Align language precisely — recruiters notice when your resume mirrors their vocabulary. When done, tell me: (1) the risk in my application as it stands, and (2) whether I should apply or address a gap before submitting.
```
*ATS optimisation for one specific application — exact rewrites included, not just keyword suggestions.*

## 10. Cover Letter

*The document that decides whether a recruiter reads your resume carefully or scans it.*

```
ROLE: You are a professional cover letter writer who has written letters that got candidates hired at Google, McKinsey, early-stage startups, and creative agencies. You know that the best cover letters do not summarise the resume — they add a layer the resume cannot.

CONTEXT:
- My name: [name]
- Role I am applying for: [job title at company]
- Why I want this specific role (honest version): [what excites you about it]
- Why I want this specific company (beyond "great culture"): [something specific you know about them]
- My most relevant achievement for this role: [the one thing that proves I can do this job]
- My most relevant skill: [the skill most needed for this role]
- Tone I want: [formal / conversational / confident / enthusiastic — pick one]

TASK:
1. Write a 3-paragraph cover letter — under 300 words total
2. Paragraph 1: An opening that immediately hooks the reader — reference something specific about the company or role, and make a clear statement of why you are the right fit. Do NOT start with "I am writing to apply for..."
3. Paragraph 2: One specific achievement that directly proves you can do this job — told as a brief story with a result
4. Paragraph 3: Why this specific company, not just this role — show you have done your research. End with a confident, direct call to action.
5. Write an alternative opening line only — a second option in case the first feels too bold

CONSTRAINTS:
- Under 300 words — every sentence must earn its place
- Never summarise the resume — add context the resume cannot show
- First word must NOT be "I"
- Must feel like it was written for this company specifically — not a template with names swapped in

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Cover letter (full, copy-paste ready)
- Alternative opening line: [one sentence]
- One note on what makes this letter specific to this role vs. generic

> Before you start: If my reason for wanting the company feels generic, ask me one question to find the real reason. Write the achievement paragraph as a micro-story — setup, action, result. When done, tell me: (1) the weakest sentence and how to improve it, and (2) whether to send this as an email body or attachment for this type of role.
```
*A complete, specific cover letter in 3 paragraphs — written for this company, not just this role.*

## 11. LinkedIn Optimization

*Your LinkedIn profile is your resume's online twin — and it works while you sleep.*

```
ROLE: You are a LinkedIn optimisation specialist and personal branding expert who has helped professionals at all levels go from invisible to inbound — attracting recruiter messages, speaking invitations, and opportunities without applying anywhere.

CONTEXT:
- My current LinkedIn headline: [paste it]
- My current About section (first 3 lines): [paste or "it's blank"]
- My target role: [what I want to be found for]
- My industry: [field]
- What I want LinkedIn to do for me: [get recruiter messages / attract clients / build thought leadership / all three]
- One achievement I am proud of: [specific win with a number if possible]

TASK:
1. Rewrite my LinkedIn headline — keyword-rich, specific, and scroll-stopping. Maximum 220 characters.
2. Write a full LinkedIn About section — under 300 words, conversational tone, first-person, ends with a clear call to action
3. Write my "Open to Work" note (for the private recruiter-only setting) — what roles, location, and availability
4. Tell me the 5 LinkedIn profile sections most people ignore that have the highest recruiter impact
5. Write one LinkedIn post I could publish this week to signal expertise in my field — under 150 words

CONSTRAINTS:
- Headline must not just be my job title — it must be a value statement
- About section must not start with "I am a..." — open with a hook
- Every section must be optimised for LinkedIn's search algorithm — include keywords naturally
- The weekly post must be genuine and non-promotional — recruiters read posts, not ads

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Headline: [text] — [character count]
- About section: [full text]
- Open to Work note: [text]
- 5 high-impact sections: numbered list with one-line action for each
- Weekly post: [ready to publish]

> Before you start: If my current headline or target role is unclear, ask me one question. Write as if a recruiter searches for my target role at 10pm and my profile is the first thing they see. When done, tell me: (1) the one LinkedIn change that generates the most inbound messages, and (2) how often to post to build visibility without being annoying.
```
*Rewrites your LinkedIn profile to attract inbound opportunities — not just look good when someone already knows you.*

## 12. Interview Prep from Resume

*Prepare for the questions your resume will generate before you walk in.*

```
ROLE: You are an interview coach who prepares candidates for high-stakes interviews at competitive companies. You read resumes like a hiring manager — identifying the questions, doubts, and curiosity that every line will trigger.

CONTEXT:
- My resume: [paste your full resume or the most relevant sections]
- The role I am interviewing for: [job title and company]
- Interview format: [phone screen / video / in-person / panel / technical / case study]
- My biggest weakness on paper: [the thing on your resume you are most worried about being asked]
- My biggest strength on paper: [the thing you most want to be asked about]

TASK:
1. Identify the 8 most likely interview questions based on my specific resume — including the uncomfortable ones
2. Write a strong STAR-method answer for each question (Situation, Task, Action, Result) using details from my resume
3. Write a 60-second "Tell me about yourself" answer — structured as past, present, future
4. Write a confident answer to the one question I am most worried about
5. Give me 5 smart questions I should ask the interviewer — tailored to this specific company and role

CONSTRAINTS:
- Questions must be specific to my resume — not generic "what is your greatest weakness"
- STAR answers must use real details from my resume — not placeholders
- "Tell me about yourself" must be under 75 seconds when read aloud at a natural pace
- The question I'm worried about must be answered honestly — no deflection strategies

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- 8 interview questions with STAR answers: numbered, each with Q then A
- Tell me about yourself: [script with approximate timing noted]
- Answer to my worry question: [text]
- 5 questions to ask: numbered, each with [question] + [why this question impresses interviewers]

> Before you start: If my resume or target role is too vague for specific questions, ask me one question. Read my resume like a skeptical interviewer — look for the gaps and jumps. When done, tell me: (1) the question I am most likely to fumble based on my resume, and (2) how to practice these answers alone before the interview.
```
*Predicts the exact questions your resume will generate — with tailored STAR answers and smart questions to ask.*
