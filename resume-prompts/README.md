# Resume Writing Prompts — Free Copy-Paste Examples

## What is this?

This is a collection of prompts for building a complete resume using **ChatGPT**, **Claude**, or **Gemini** — organised in the exact order you would build one from scratch, section by section, top to bottom.

Start at the top (headshot and header) and work your way down. Each section has ready-made prompts you can copy, fill in, and paste into any AI tool. No experience needed.

## How to use this

1. Open [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com), [Claude](https://claude.ai), or [Gemini](https://gemini.google.com) — all free
2. Start from Section 1 if you are building from scratch, or jump to any section you need to improve
3. Copy the prompt from the code block
4. Replace everything inside `[brackets]` with your real information
5. Paste and send

> **Tip for beginners:** A resume is built top to bottom — name, photo, summary, experience, skills, education, projects. Follow this file in order and you will have a complete resume by the end.

## 1. Headshot & Profile Photo

*The photo at the top of your resume or LinkedIn profile is the first thing people see. A good headshot signals professionalism before anyone reads a single word. Use these prompts to generate one with AI or brief a photographer.*

```
Generate a professional headshot-style portrait for a [job title] in the [industry] industry. The person is [gender, approximate age range], dressed in [smart casual / business formal], standing or sitting in front of a [plain white / light grey / blurred office] background. Soft studio lighting, direct eye contact with camera, confident and approachable expression, shot on Canon 5D 85mm lens, shallow depth of field, photorealistic --ar 4:5 --v 6
```
*Use this in Midjourney or ChatGPT with DALL-E to generate a professional headshot. Replace the details with your own description — or use it as a reference photo brief for a photographer.*

```
I need a professional headshot for my resume and LinkedIn profile. I am a [job title] in [industry]. Describe exactly what I should wear, what background to use, what expression to have, and how the photo should be framed. Also tell me: should I include a photo on my resume for jobs in [country]?
```
*Gives you a complete brief for taking your own headshot with a phone or camera — and tells you whether a photo is expected in your target job market (it varies by country).*

```
I have a casual photo of myself. Write a detailed prompt I can paste into an AI image tool (Midjourney or DALL-E) to transform it into a professional headshot suitable for a [job title] resume and LinkedIn profile.
```
*Helps you turn an existing photo description into a polished AI-generated headshot prompt.*

## 2. Resume Header & Contact Info

*The header is the very top of your resume — your name, professional title, and contact details. It sounds simple but it sets the tone and is often done wrong.*

```
Write the header section for my resume. My name is [Name]. I am a [job title / professional title]. My contact details are: email [email], phone [number], LinkedIn [URL or username], portfolio/GitHub [URL if applicable], location [City, Country — no full address needed].

Format it cleanly for a modern resume. Tell me: should I include my full address, or just city and country? Should I include a photo? What should my professional title say?
```
*Builds your entire resume header and answers the common questions people get wrong — like whether to include a full address (usually no) or a photo (depends on country).*

```
My current resume title under my name says "[paste current title]". I am applying for [target role] at [company type]. Rewrite my professional title to be more specific, keyword-rich, and relevant to the role I want.
```
*Optimises the professional title line under your name — most people write something too vague like "Experienced Professional" when they should write something like "Senior Product Designer — Mobile & SaaS."*

## 3. Professional Summary

*The summary is 3–5 lines at the top of your resume, just below the header. It is the first thing a recruiter reads and the most important piece of writing on the page.*

```
I am a [job title] applying for a [target role] at a [company type]. Write my resume summary in 3–4 sentences that highlights my [top skill], [second skill], and [key achievement]. Make it ATS-friendly and avoid clichés like "results-driven" or "passionate."
```
*Writes your summary section — the 3–4 lines that appear at the very top of your resume. Fill in your real skills and one specific achievement for the best result.*

```
I have [X] years of experience in [field] but I am switching careers to [new field]. Write a resume summary that bridges my existing skills to the new role, highlights transferable experience, and does not draw attention to the career change.
```
*For career changers — frames your past experience as relevant to your new direction without making the gap obvious.*

```
Write a professional summary for a recent graduate with a degree in [subject] from [University], a GPA of [X], and internship experience at [Company]. They are applying for entry-level [role] positions. Keep it confident despite limited experience.
```
*For students and fresh graduates who are not sure what to write when they don't have years of experience yet.*

```
Here is my current resume summary: "[paste it]". Rewrite it to be more specific, results-focused, and compelling. Remove any filler phrases and make every sentence earn its place.
```
*Improves an existing summary — paste yours in and get a tighter, stronger version back.*

## 4. Work Experience & Bullet Points

*The work experience section is the heart of your resume. Each role needs strong, achievement-focused bullet points — not a list of duties.*

```
Write a professional work experience entry for my role as [Job Title] at [Company] from [start date] to [end date]. My main responsibilities were [list 3–5 things you did]. Rewrite these as achievement-focused bullet points using the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) with specific metrics where possible.
```
*Turns a list of job duties into strong, achievement-focused bullets — the single biggest improvement most resumes need.*

```
Write a professional resume for a [job title] with [X] years of experience in [industry]. Include work experience at [Company A] and [Company B], a skills section, and education. Use strong action verbs and quantify achievements throughout.
```
*Builds your full work experience section from scratch — great starting point if you are writing your first resume or starting over.*

```
Rewrite these resume bullet points to be more impactful with quantified results: [paste your bullets here]. Use strong action verbs and include percentages, dollar amounts, or timeframes where relevant.
```
*Example: paste in "Managed social media accounts" and get back "Grew Instagram following by 40% in 3 months through a consistent daily content strategy."*

```
My resume bullet says: "[paste weak bullet]". Rewrite it to sound more impressive while staying truthful. Add a measurable result if one can be reasonably inferred from the context.
```
*Improves one bullet at a time — useful when you want to strengthen specific lines without rewriting everything.*

```
Turn these job duties into achievement-focused resume bullets. Make each one start with a different strong action verb: [paste duties list].
```
*For when your current resume reads like a job description ("Responsible for...") instead of a list of wins.*

```
I managed a team and improved our process but I do not have exact numbers. Write 3 resume bullets that sound credible and strong without fabricating specific metrics.
```
*For people who know they did good work but can't quantify it — honest bullets that still sound impactful.*

```
Rewrite my entire work experience section. Here is what I currently have: [paste experience section]. Make it tighter, results-focused, and optimised for ATS keyword scanning.
```
*A full overhaul of your work history in one go — paste in everything and get it back improved.*

## 5. Skills Section

*The skills section lists what you know — tools, software, languages, and key competencies. It needs to be relevant and readable, not a random wall of buzzwords.*

```
I have experience in [list your tools, software, and skills]. Organise these into a clean skills section for a [job title] resume. Group them into categories like Technical Skills, Soft Skills, and Tools. Remove anything that is too basic or irrelevant for the role.
```
*Turns a messy list of skills into something structured and intentional — and cuts the things that actually hurt your resume.*

```
What are the top 10 skills I should list on a resume for a [job title] role at a [company type]? Focus on what recruiters and ATS systems look for in [year].
```
*Tells you exactly which skills to include for your specific role — so you don't waste space on things that don't matter.*

```
I am a [job title]. Write a skills section that balances hard skills like [skill1, skill2] with soft skills like [skill3, skill4], formatted for a clean modern resume layout.
```
*For people who only list technical skills and forget that communication, leadership, and problem-solving also belong on a resume.*

## 6. Education Section

*The education section lists your degrees, certifications, and relevant training. Where it sits on your resume — near the top or near the bottom — depends on how much experience you have.*

```
Write the education section for my resume. I studied [degree name] at [University], graduating in [year] with a GPA of [X / distinction / pass]. I also completed [any relevant certifications or courses]. I am applying for [role type].

Tell me: should my education section go before or after my work experience? Should I include my GPA?
```
*Builds your education entry and answers the common questions — like whether to include GPA (usually only if it is strong and you are a recent graduate).*

```
I have a degree in [subject] but I am applying for a role in [different field]. Write my education entry in a way that highlights the transferable skills and relevant coursework rather than the degree title itself.
```
*For people whose degree doesn't directly match the job — reframes it to show relevant skills instead.*

```
I have no degree but I have completed [online courses / bootcamps / certifications] in [subjects]. Write an education section that presents these credibly and professionally alongside any other qualifications I have.
```
*For self-taught professionals and bootcamp graduates — positions non-traditional education as a strength, not a gap.*

```
I have multiple certifications: [list them]. Which ones should I include on my resume for a [target role], and how should I format them? Write the education section.
```
*Helps you decide which certifications are worth listing and formats them correctly.*

## 7. Projects Section

*A projects section is especially important for students, career changers, developers, designers, and anyone who wants to show real-world work beyond their job titles.*

```
Write a projects section for my resume. I want to include the following projects: [describe each project briefly — what it was, what you built or did, what tools you used, and any results]. I am applying for [target role].

For each project, write a 2–3 line description that highlights the skills used and the outcome.
```
*Builds your entire projects section from descriptions — shows recruiters real work, not just job titles.*

```
I built [describe your project]. Write a resume project description for it that would impress a recruiter hiring for a [target role]. Highlight the technical skills used, the problem it solved, and any measurable outcomes.
```
*Writes one strong project entry — especially useful for developers, designers, and anyone with a portfolio.*

```
I have these projects: [list them]. I am applying for [role]. Which 3 should I include on my resume and why? What should I emphasise in each description?
```
*Helps you choose the right projects to feature — not everything you have built belongs on every resume.*

## 8. Handling Gaps & Tricky Situations

*Use these if you have employment gaps, career changes, unusual history, or situations you are not sure how to present.*

```
I have a [X]-year gap in my resume from [year] to [year]. During that time I was [briefly explain: traveling / caregiving / freelancing / studying / dealing with health]. Write 2 ways to address this honestly and positively — one for the resume itself, one for a cover letter.
```
*Addressed honestly, gaps rarely matter as much as people fear. A prepared, confident explanation removes all awkwardness.*

```
I was laid off from my last role. How should I frame this on my resume, in my cover letter, and on LinkedIn without raising red flags? Write example language for each.
```
*Layoffs are very common — a clear, calm answer removes any stigma immediately.*

```
I have done freelance and contract work for the last [X] years with no single full-time employer. How should I structure this period on my resume so it looks strong and deliberate rather than unstable?
```
*For freelancers and contractors who worry their experience pattern looks inconsistent.*

```
I worked at the same company for [X] years and was promoted [X] times. How do I show multiple roles at the same company to clearly show career progression?
```
*For long-tenure employees — stacking roles at one company correctly shows growth, not stagnation.*

## 9. Tailoring for a Specific Job

*Once your resume is built, this section helps you customise it for each application — to match keywords, pass ATS filters, and stand out to a specific employer.*

```
Here is a job description: [paste JD]. Here is my current resume: [paste resume]. Rewrite my summary and top 3 bullet points to align with the keywords and requirements in this job description.
```
*The most powerful thing you can do before submitting — many companies filter resumes by keyword before a human ever reads them.*

```
Extract the top 10 keywords from this job description that I should include in my resume: [paste JD].
```
*A quick first step — find the exact words the employer is looking for so you can work them in naturally.*

```
I am applying to [Company Name]. Based on what you know about their culture and values, rewrite my resume summary to reflect what they look for in candidates.
```
*For targeting specific well-known companies — shows you have done your research.*

```
My resume is for a [role] in [industry]. Rewrite it to pass ATS screening for this job description: [paste JD]. Highlight keyword matches and tell me what to add.
```
*ATS optimisation — tells you exactly what your resume is missing before you submit.*

```
Compare my resume to this job description and tell me: (1) what matches well, (2) what is missing, (3) what to cut. Resume: [paste]. JD: [paste].
```
*A fast gap analysis before applying to a role you really want.*

## 10. Cover Letter

*Your cover letter goes with your resume when you apply. It should not repeat your resume — it should add context, show personality, and give the employer a reason to choose you.*

```
Write a cover letter for a [job title] position at [Company]. I have [X] years of experience in [field] and my top achievements include [achievement 1] and [achievement 2]. Keep it to 3 paragraphs — confident but not arrogant.
```
*A complete cover letter you can customise per application — fill in your real achievements for the best result.*

```
I am applying to [Company] for a [role]. Write a cover letter opening paragraph that immediately grabs attention and shows I understand their product, mission, or problem they are solving.
```
*The hardest part — most cover letters open with "I am writing to apply for..." which no one remembers. This prompt fixes that.*

```
Write a cover letter for someone switching from [old career] to [new career]. Address the career change directly and reframe past experience as an advantage for the new role.
```
*For career changers — own the transition rather than hoping the reader doesn't notice.*

## 11. LinkedIn Optimization

*Your LinkedIn profile works alongside your resume. Recruiters search LinkedIn — a strong profile means opportunities come to you, not just the other way around.*

```
Rewrite my LinkedIn headline for a [job title] who specialises in [skill] and is open to [type of opportunities]. Make it keyword-rich and compelling. Current headline: [paste].
```
*The most important line on your LinkedIn — recruiters see it before anything else. Most people write their job title; this prompt makes it a search-optimised value statement.*

```
Write a LinkedIn About section for a [job title] with [X] years of experience. Include: what I do, who I help, my key achievements, and a call to action. Under 300 words, conversational tone.
```
*The About section most people leave blank or fill with generic phrases — a good one gets recruiters to reach out.*

```
Turn my resume bullet points into LinkedIn experience descriptions. Make them slightly more conversational and human — LinkedIn is not a formal document: [paste bullets].
```
*LinkedIn descriptions should feel different from a resume — warmer and more direct.*

## 12. Interview Prep from Resume

*Once your resume is out, prepare for what comes next — the questions your resume will generate in an interview.*

```
Based on my resume below, generate 10 interview questions a hiring manager is likely to ask me and write strong STAR-method answers for each: [paste resume].
```
*ChatGPT reads your actual resume and predicts exactly what tough questions you will face — then prepares answers tailored to your experience.*

```
What weaknesses or gaps on my resume might a recruiter notice and ask about? How should I address each proactively in an interview? Resume: [paste].
```
*Prepares you for the uncomfortable questions before they catch you off guard.*

```
Write a 60-second "Tell me about yourself" answer I can use in an interview, based on my resume: [paste resume].
```
*The most common interview opener — most people ramble. This gives you a tight, confident answer.*
