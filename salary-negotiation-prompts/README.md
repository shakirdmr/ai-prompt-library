# Salary Negotiation Prompts — 50 Free Copy-Paste Examples

## What is this?

This is a collection of prompts for negotiating your salary — whether you have a new job offer, want to ask for a raise, or need to handle pushback from a manager or recruiter.

Most people never negotiate — and it costs them tens of thousands of dollars over a career. The reason is usually not confidence, it is not knowing what to say. These prompts give you the exact words. You just fill in your numbers and situation.

## The 5-Part Prompt Structure

Every prompt here uses a professional framework used by experienced AI practitioners:

| Part | What it does |
|---|---|
| **ROLE** | Tells the AI who to be — a negotiation expert, not a generalist |
| **CONTEXT** | Gives the AI the specific details it needs about your situation |
| **TASK** | Tells the AI exactly what to produce |
| **CONSTRAINTS** | Sets the rules — tone, length, what to avoid |
| **OUTPUT FORMAT** | Specifies exactly how the answer should be structured |

**Plus a power footer** — each prompt ends with an instruction that triggers the AI to ask clarifying questions, think step by step, and suggest what to do next. This is the difference between an average AI user and a power user.

## How to use this

1. Open [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com) or [Claude](https://claude.ai) — both free to use
2. Find the section that matches your situation
3. Copy the full prompt from the code block
4. Replace everything inside `[brackets]` with your real information
5. Read the output, adapt it to your own voice, and then send it or say it

> **Tip for beginners:** Salary negotiation feels uncomfortable because most people think it will offend the employer. It almost never does. Hiring managers expect it. The worst they can say is no — and even then, you are no worse off than if you hadn't asked. These prompts help you ask professionally so there is no awkwardness.

## Research & Preparation

*Use these before any negotiation conversation. Knowing your market value is the most important step.*

&nbsp;

**1.**
```
ROLE: You are a compensation analyst with access to salary benchmarking data across industries and geographies, and you help professionals understand their true market value before entering any negotiation.

CONTEXT:
- My job title: [e.g. Product Manager]
- Years of total experience: [X]
- Years in this specific type of role: [X]
- Location or remote: [e.g. London / fully remote, US-based]
- Industry: [e.g. B2B SaaS]
- Company size I am targeting: [e.g. 50-500 employees / enterprise]
- Year: [current year]

TASK:
1. Give me a salary range for my role at my level in my location
2. Explain what factors push compensation to the top vs. bottom of that range
3. List 3 reliable sources I can use to verify or update this data

CONSTRAINTS:
- Be specific about geography — remote roles at US companies often pay differently than in-office roles
- Break out base salary, bonus, and equity separately if they apply to my level
- If my information is too vague to give a tight range, ask me one clarifying question

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Base salary range: [bottom — top, with a typical midpoint]
- What drives you to the top: [3-4 specific factors]
- What drives you to the bottom: [3-4 specific factors]
- Sources to check: [3 named sources with what they are best for]

> Before you start: If anything in my context is missing or unclear, ask me one specific question first. Then work through your answer step by step. When done, tell me: (1) one piece of information that would make your answer even stronger, and (2) the best next prompt I should run after this one.
```
*Knowing your market range before any negotiation is the single most important preparation step — negotiating without a number is like haggling without knowing the fair price.*

*Understanding what to negotiate for — comparing total compensation packages is more complex than comparing salaries, especially when equity, bonuses, and benefits differ significantly.*

## Handling Pushback

*Use these when the employer says no, not now, or pushes back on your ask.*

&nbsp;

**16.**
```

2. Get a specific commitment: a date, a target, or a criteria for revisiting
3. Give me language to use if I have external options I can ethically reference

- Do not accept a vague "we'll revisit" without a date — that is the same as no
- Stay professional and collaborative — do not signal frustration

- Immediate verbal response: [what to say in the meeting — 2-3 sentences]
- The specific ask: [one sentence that gets a date or criteria commitment]
- If I have external options: [how to mention them ethically — exact phrasing]
- Follow-up email after the meeting: [confirm the timeline in writing — under 60 words]

```
*"Budget frozen" situations — always get a specific date to revisit, not a vague "later." A vague future promise is worth nothing without a specific date and agreed criteria.*
