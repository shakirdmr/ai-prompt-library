# Salary Negotiation Prompts — 50 Free Copy-Paste Examples

## What is this?

This is a collection of 50 prompts for negotiating your salary, whether you have a new job offer, want to ask for a raise, or need to handle pushback from a manager or recruiter.

Most people never negotiate — and it costs them tens of thousands of dollars over a career. The reason is usually not confidence, it is not knowing what to say. These prompts give you the exact words. You just fill in your numbers and situation.

## How to use this

1. Open [ChatGPT](https://chat.openai.com) or [Claude](https://claude.ai) — both free to use
2. Find the section that matches your situation
3. Copy the prompt from the code block
4. Replace everything inside `[brackets]` with your real information
5. Paste it into the AI chat, read the output, and adapt it to your own voice before sending

> **Tip for beginners:** Salary negotiation feels uncomfortable because most people think it will offend the employer. It almost never does. Hiring managers expect it. The worst they can say is no — and even then, you are no worse off than if you hadn't asked. These prompts help you ask professionally so there is no awkwardness.

## Research & Preparation

*Use these before any negotiation conversation. Knowing your market value is the most important step.*

```
I am a [job title] with [X] years of experience in [city/country]. What is the market salary range for my role in [year]? What sources should I check and what factors affect the range?
```
> Good for: establishing your baseline before any negotiation — you need a number to anchor on.

```
I have a job offer for [role] at [company type] paying [amount]. Is this competitive for [city] in [year]? What should I counter with?
```
> Good for: a quick sanity check on whether an offer is fair, and what a reasonable counter looks like.

```
Help me build a case for a salary negotiation. My role is [title], I have been at the company [X] years, I earn [current salary], and the market rate is [market rate]. Write a structured argument.
```
> Good for: preparing talking points before a raise conversation with your manager.

```
What are 5 things I should research before negotiating my salary for a [role] at a [company size] company?
```
> Good for: first-time negotiators who want a checklist before they walk into the conversation.

```
I am switching from [current role, current salary] to [new role]. What salary should I target and how much increase is reasonable to ask for?
```
> Good for: understanding what jump is realistic when changing jobs so you don't ask for too little or too much.

## Initial Offer Negotiation

*Use these when you receive a job offer and want to negotiate it higher before accepting.*

```
I received a job offer of [amount] for a [role] at [Company]. I was hoping for [target amount]. Write a professional email to negotiate higher without sounding ungrateful or aggressive.
```
> Example: "I received an offer of $75,000 for a marketing manager role. I was hoping for $85,000." — ChatGPT will write a polished counter-offer email you can send immediately.

```
Write a script for a 5-minute phone call where I counter a job offer of [amount] and ask for [target amount]. I have [X] years of experience and a competing offer of [amount]. Keep it confident but collaborative.
```
> Good for: if the negotiation is happening by phone — having a script means you won't freeze or forget your key points.

```
The recruiter asked me what salary I am looking for before giving me a number. Write 3 different responses I can use to deflect and ask them to go first.
```
> Good for: the classic recruiter tactic of asking your number first — whoever names a number first is usually at a disadvantage.

```
A company gave me an offer but said the salary is fixed and non-negotiable. Write a script to negotiate other parts of the compensation package: signing bonus, extra PTO, remote work, earlier review date.
```
> Good for: when the base salary truly can't move — there is often much more flexibility in other benefits.

```
Write a counter-offer email. Offer received: [amount]. My target: [amount]. Reason: I have [competing offer / market data / X years experience]. Keep it under 150 words and professional.
```
> Good for: a short, direct counter-offer email — brief and confident is more effective than long and over-explained.

## Asking for a Raise

*Use these when you want to ask your current employer for more money.*

```
Write a script for asking my manager for a salary increase. I have been in this role for [X] years, I earn [current amount], market rate is [market rate], and my recent achievements include [achievement 1] and [achievement 2].
```
> Good for: the annual or mid-year raise conversation — give ChatGPT your real achievements and salary for the most tailored result.

```
I want to ask for a [X]% raise but my company had a tough year. Write a persuasive script that acknowledges the situation but still makes a strong case based on my contributions.
```
> Good for: asking for a raise during a difficult business period — framing matters enormously here.

```
Help me prepare for a performance review where I plan to ask for a raise. My role: [title]. Key wins this year: [list 3]. What should I say and how should I frame the ask?
```
> Good for: structuring your performance review conversation so it naturally leads to a salary discussion.

```
Write an email to my manager requesting a meeting to discuss my compensation. Keep it brief, professional, and do not reveal the exact ask in the email itself.
```
> Good for: setting up the conversation — never make the ask over email or Slack if you can avoid it; ask in person or on a call.

```
My manager said they will think about the raise request and get back to me. It has been 3 weeks. Write a polite follow-up email.
```
> Good for: following up without appearing pushy — silence is often just busy, not a no.

## Handling Pushback

*Use these when the employer says no, not now, or pushes back on your ask.*

```
My manager said the budget is frozen and they cannot give me a raise right now. Write a professional response that keeps the conversation open and sets up a timeline for revisiting it.
```
> Good for: "no for now" situations — always get a specific date to revisit, not a vague "later."

```
The hiring manager said my counter-offer is too high. Write a response that holds my position while showing flexibility on non-cash benefits.
```
> Good for: when they push back but haven't said a hard no — there is usually room to meet in the middle.

```
My employer said they are already paying me at the top of the band. Write a professional response that challenges this politely and asks for transparency on the band range.
```
> Good for: "top of band" is a common negotiation tactic — you have a right to ask for the actual range.

```
I was told I need to wait until my next performance review in 6 months for a raise. Write a response that gets a written commitment and an agreed target.
```
> Good for: turning "wait until your review" into a concrete, documented commitment with clear criteria.

```
The recruiter came back with only a small increase from their original offer. Write a response that pushes one more time without risking the offer.
```
> Good for: when the first counter got a tiny bump — one more respectful push often works.

## Total Compensation

*Use these when the salary alone doesn't tell the full story — equity, bonuses, and benefits are often negotiable too.*

```
A company offered me [base salary] but I currently earn [total comp including bonus and equity]. Write a script to negotiate total compensation, not just base salary.
```
> Good for: tech or finance roles where total compensation is much more complex than just the base salary.

```
Explain the difference between base salary, total cash compensation, and total compensation including equity. Write a script where I use this knowledge to negotiate a better overall package.
```
> Good for: beginners who have never dealt with equity, RSUs, or bonus structures before.

```
I want to negotiate a signing bonus to offset the unvested equity I am leaving behind at my current job. Write an email explaining this and asking for a specific amount.
```
> Good for: when you are leaving unvested stock — companies often cover this with a signing bonus if you ask.

```
Write a script to negotiate for 100% remote work as part of my offer. Frame it in terms of productivity and what I bring to the role.
```
> Good for: remote work is often negotiable even when the job is listed as hybrid or in-office.

```
I want to negotiate an earlier performance review date (3 months instead of 12). Write a script that ties this to clear performance targets.
```
> Good for: when you accept a lower offer with the expectation of a quick raise — get that review date in writing.

## Specific Scenarios

*Use these for situations that don't fit the standard scripts.*

```
I am a woman negotiating my salary in a male-dominated industry. Write a confident, assertive negotiation script that does not apologize for the ask.
```
> Good for: a direct, strong approach that addresses the specific challenge of negotiating without being second-guessed.

```
I am a recent graduate with no prior salary negotiation experience. Write a simple, friendly but firm script to negotiate my first offer up by [amount].
```
> Good for: first-job negotiation — even entry-level salaries are often negotiable by $2,000–$5,000.

```
I have two competing job offers. Write a script to use one offer to improve the other without damaging either relationship.
```
> Good for: the most powerful negotiation leverage you can have — use it professionally and it almost always works.

```
I am a freelancer transitioning to full-time employment. My freelance rate was [amount/hour]. Write a script to convert this to a competitive full-time salary ask.
```
> Good for: freelancers often undercharge when going full-time because they forget to factor in benefits, taxes, and stability.

```
I want to negotiate a remote work stipend, gym membership, and learning budget as part of my package. Write a single email that asks for all three professionally.
```
> Good for: benefits negotiation — most people forget these are negotiable and they add up significantly over a year.
